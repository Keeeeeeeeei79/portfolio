ICL 9230 · Contemporary Leadership and Management
Understanding Leadership Theories and Their Real-World Application
Transformational LeadershipSituational LeadershipWorkplace Practice
Learning
Leadership Theories
Transformational & situational leadership frameworks studied in ICL 9230.
Application
Café Team Management
Adjusting leadership style based on team member experience and readiness in day-to-day work.
Outcome
Conscious Leadership Skills
Improved team communication and a deliberate, theory-informed approach to working with others.

What I have learnt probably most from the ICL Leading and Management course is the discussion about the key leadership theories, in particular, transformational and situational leadership. Before this course, I have observed the effect of leadership action for several years (as a physiotherapist in Japan), but I did not have a framework to link my observations as to why some leaders were effective and others not, or why I could change the leadership style.

We studied how transformational leaders can inspire people by referring to core values and creating a vision that inspires, and how situational leaders can change their style of leadership to the needs and readiness of each team member. This difference spoke to my heart. Looking back, in my clinical career, I would now have a list of the leaders I like and dislike and can understand through these models why certain leadership styles were effective or ineffective in a particular scenario.

The aspect of this learning that was most useful was that it could be transferred to my current Auckland workplace. Being a cafe worker, I began to apply the situational leadership theories: new staff should be given more directives and structure while experienced staff can be given freedom. I found that this technique improved communication and reduced tensions in the workplace during busy periods.

I plan to continue to use these theories in the future as I will be involved in business. Understanding leadership from a conceptual level and have the ability to reflect on and modify their own style is important not only for managers but for those who aspire to have a great impact on an organisation. This has shaped my future career aspirations where I will be in the marketing and data analytics industry where in most cases it is required to manage others without having power over them.

Application

Outcome
Improved Team Relationships
Better professional relationships and greater empathy toward colleagues from other cultural backgrounds.

The significance of communication, the cross-cultural communication in particular, in managing a team effectively was another lesson that I learnt from ICL studies. In the Leading and Management class, we discussed how different communication styles, high or low context, direct or indirect communication can lead to miscommunication in various teams.

For me, this is a very relevant issue as I am a Japanese working and living in New Zealand. I am from a high-context culture, meaning that we speak less and infer much, and was faced with some difficulties with more direct communication styles in New Zealand. This course gave me a framework and a vocabulary to understand these differences not as deficiencies, but rather as different cultural norms that need to be recognised and the gap between them must be closed.

This learning was helpful at work. I was more careful to express my thoughts, rather than suggesting or keeping silent so as not to cause any trouble, like in the Japanese working style. I also became more tolerant with other colleagues who might be from a different culture and may have the same challenges.

I think it's a very important skill for my future job. As a data analyst and marketer in New Zealand, I will be working with various teams on a regular basis and will be sharing complex information with the stakeholders with varying levels of technical skills. I am also in the process of building the skill of being able to adapt to different people and this course gave me the conceptual background to achieve that.

One of the lessons that have stayed with me for a long time is to set my goals in a formalised manner, i.e. use of such concepts as SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound). This is one of the concepts that I learnt during my ICL studies and it is a simple enough concept, but the application of it in my own development was unknown.

Previously my approach to career planning was laidback and reactive. I had the desire to do more and change direction, but I didn't know what I wanted to do: I want to work in business or I want to learn more about data. As I have had to go through the process of setting specific learning goals (as is required for this portfolio), this has forced me to be more reflective in terms of what I want, and what can be done to get there.

This shift in mentality has proven to be useful. I now know how to better manage my study time by setting goals, such as working on a digital marketing certification, and how to better use the application Excel to manage data. Rather than being paralysed by the idea that there is so much that I don't know, I now feel like I am on the right path and making progress.

In my future, strategic planning and goal-setting are skills that I find to be relevant to the jobs that I will be applying for. A marketing analyst has to set clear and measurable objectives for marketing campaigns; a business analyst must set clear and measurable outcomes for a project. These are the skills that I am working on as a student, which will set the stage for how I will work as an employee.

Connecting Academic Theory to Workplace Practice

Learning
Reflective Practice
Applying management theory as an analytical lens for observing real organisational behaviour.
Application
Café Observation
Analysing how management style and culture affect team morale and communication patterns at work.
Outcome

One key lesson of my ICL course work has been how to make the connections between the theoretical and practical aspects of my practice a reality. I am a Japanese clinical healthcare graduate, and was trained to follow evidence-based practice, but the connection between the science of management and the workplace behaviour was not obvious to me when I started to study business.

The Leading and Management course did not only want me to learn the theories: the Leading and Management course also made me look at the real organisational practices, observe, and reflect on the organisational practices from the theoretical point of view. For example, when I was reading about the organisational culture, and the impacts of the organisational culture in the motivation of the employees, I was critically reflecting about the cafe where I work: how the manager's communicational style with the employees impacts on the team spirit, how the informal norms of the organisation affect who speaks and who doesn't.

This reflection exercise has been very valuable. It has made my course work relevant and tangible and not theoretical, and my work more analytical. I am now more observant in my workplaces and I need to ask myself why things are the way they are, and how can they be improved.

This ability to connect theory with practice will be important in my journey towards marketing or data analytics. Data is never in isolation; it is important to understand the organisational and human context to interpret relevant data. One of these examples, which I have learnt from ICL, is to practice theory-to-practice thinking that I will continue to pursue in my professional life.

Learning

Application

Outcome

Perhaps the least apparent, but, ultimately, least insignificant is the fact that being in New Zealand has allowed me to become more resilient and flexible overall. Moving to a new country, studying in a new language, working in the hospitality industry and at the same time having to juggle studying for my degree, and adapting to a new work culture, environment, and country: all these aspects have made me become more flexible than I have ever thought I would be.

Studying ICL, I have understood that being resilient is not just a personal trait, but also a work skill. The business literature is increasingly recognising the need for individuals who can deal with uncertainty, who are able to bounce back after facing adversity and/or who perform well under pressure. This has been a great opportunity for me to experience resilience in action.

This education has been useful in a number of ways. It has also been an education academically that has taught me to "stay the course" with the difficult work and ask for help where needed rather than suffer in silence as would be expected in Japan. It is a lesson in a work environment that has also given me anecdotes to use and explain in the work applications and interviews that I can adapt and work in different cultural and work settings.

One example is, in particular, my attitude to feedback. At the beginning of my schooling it was embarrassing to receive feedback on my work and the feedback was negative. Over time, I have come to see feedback as an opportunity to improve and I am seeking out feedback. This shift in my attitude, that is, seeing the challenge as a threat and opportunity, I believe, is a key to my future success, particularly in the transition into a new career and industry.
