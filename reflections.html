Understanding Leadership Theories and Their Real-World Application

Improved team communication and a deliberate, theory-informed approach to working with others.

Among the most significant things I have learned throughout the ICL Leading and Management course is the analysis of the main theories of leadership, especially, transformational and situational leadership. Prior to this course, I had seen the action of leadership over the years, as I worked as a physiotherapist in Japan, but I never had a formal structure on which to base my observations of why certain leaders were more successful than others, or how the style of leadership could be purposefully changed.

During the course, we examined the ways transformational leaders can inspire people by referencing to shared values and inspiring a common vision, as well as how situational leaders can modify their approach to the capabilities and readiness of every team member. This difference spoke to my heart. In retrospect, my experience in the clinical career, I would now be able to have a list of leaders I had like or disliked and realize through these models why some forms of management were better or worse in one or another circumstance.

The particular aspect of this learning that was particularly helpful was that it could be applied to my present-day workplace in Auckland right away. As a cafe employee, I started to actively use the situational leadership principles: the new team members should be offered more guidance and structure, whereas more experienced employees could be given more freedom. I observed that this strategy enhanced communication and minimized frustrations within the team when there was a rush of business.

I intend to keep using these theories in the future since I will be in business settings. Knowing leadership conceptually, and having the capacity to introspect and change style, is not only pertinent to managers, but to anyone who has aspirations of making a significant contribution to an organisation. This has influenced my future career thoughts where I will be in the field of marketing and data analytics where in most cases it is necessary to influence others without having official power.

The importance of communication, and the cross-cultural communication in particular in the efficient team management was another important lesson that I gained during my ICL studies. During the Leading and Management course, we explored how various communication styles, either high or low context, direct or indirect communication may cause misunderstanding in various teams.

This touched me personally as a Japanese professional working and living in New Zealand. As a high-context cultural background, and therefore one in which very little is said and a lot is implied, I was initially challenged by the more direct manner of communication in New Zealand. The course provided me with a language and a structure to interpret those differences not as lacks, but as different cultural norms that must be identified and closed the gap between them.

Practically, this learning was beneficial to me at work. I was more conscious of making my thoughts clear and explicit, instead of insinuating or remaining silent in order to not cause a commotion, which is typical of the Japanese work culture. I also learned to be more understanding of other colleagues who may have a different cultural background and may have the same adjustment issues.

I consider it a very essential skill in my future career. Working in data analytics and marketing in New Zealand, I will engage with different teams on a regular basis and convey complicated information to the stakeholders with different levels of technical expertise. I am also in the process of developing the competency of adjusting my communication style in response to various audiences, and this course provided me with a solid conceptual framework with which to accomplish it.

Among the lessons that have left a long-lasting impression on me, there is the habit of setting goals in a structured form, namely, the application of such frameworks as SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound). This is one concept that I came across in my ICL studies and the concept is fairly simple, but the field of using it in my own growth was something completely unfamiliar.

Before my studies, my career planning style was carefree and responsive. My intentions to do more and shift gears were there, but my ambitions were unclear: I want to work in business or I want to learn more about data. As I had to go through the process of stating particular, quantifiable learning objectives, as is needed within this portfolio, this compelled me to be more thoughtful with regard to what I actually want, and what actions will practically bring me there.

This change of thought has already come in handy. I have begun to better prioritize my learning time by setting specific goals, like passing a digital marketing certification or learning better how to use Excel to handle data. Instead of believing that there is so much that I do not know, I now have a better road map and progress seems possible.

In the future, strategic thinking and goal-setting are competencies that I find to be directly applicable to the kind of jobs I will be seeking. A marketing analyst must establish quantifiable campaign goals; a business analyst must establish quantifiable project outcomes. These are the habits that I am practicing now, as a student, which will lay the groundwork on how I will perform in the future as a professional.

An important lesson of my ICL coursework has been the ability to understand how to make the gap between theoretical and practical aspects of my practice a reality. As a Japanese clinical healthcare graduate, I was conditioned to use evidence-based guidelines, however, the relationship between management science and the daily workplace behaviour seemed less obvious to me when I started my business education.

The Leading and Management course did not just want me to learn the theories: the Leading and Management course also made me observe, analyse and consider the real organisational behaviour in the light of the theoretical perspectives. To illustrate, when I was reading about organisational culture and the role of organisational culture in the motivation of employees, I was critically thinking about the cafe where I am employed, how the way the manager communicates with employees affects the morale of the team, how the informal norms affect who is willing to speak up and who is not.

This reflection practice has been truly useful. It has made my course work seem real and grounded and not abstract, and has made my day-to-day work more analytically rich. Now I am more attentive in my workplaces and I have to question myself why some dynamics are the way they are, and how they could be better.

This skill to relate frameworks to actual situations will be critical as I strive towards a career in marketing or data analysis. Salient data is not in a vacuum; it is crucial to know the human and organisational context in order to interpret it. One such habit, which I have acquired during ICL, is theory-to-practice thinking which I will maintain throughout my career.

Building Resilience and Adaptability as an International Student

Learning

Application
Reframing Feedback
Moving from discomfort with critical feedback to actively seeking it as a tool for growth.
Outcome
Growth Mindset
A more confident and adaptive approach to challenge , both in study and in the workplace.

Probably the least obvious, yet, in the end, the least minor one is the fact that being in New Zealand has helped me become more resilient and adaptable in general. Relocating to a foreign country, pursuing my education in a foreign language, being employed in the hospitality industry and at the same time having to cope with the challenges of my academic studies, and dealing with a completely new work environment, culture, and environment: all these factors have forced me to develop a degree of flexibility that I had not expected to have to rely on.

As a student of ICL, I have realized that resilience is not only a personal characteristic, but a professional skill. The management literature is increasingly appreciating individuals who are able to cope with uncertainty, who are able to rebound after encountering setbacks and still perform highly when under pressure. This experience has provided me with a rich, hands-on experience of what resilience is in practice.

This education has proved handy in several folds. It has also taught me academically to persevere with the challenging tasks and seek assistance when necessary instead of enduring silently, as I would have done in Japan. It is an experience professionally that has provided me with real-life examples that I can use in job applications and interviews to show that I can fit and work in various cultural and work-related situations.

One particular example has been in my attitude towards feedback. During my initial years at school, it was awkward to get feedback about my work and the feedback was often negative. Gradually, I have redefined feedback as a means of improvement, and I am now pursuing it. This change of attitude, that is, perceiving challenge as a threat and as a chance, is a shift that I believe is a key to long-term personal success especially as I maneuver through a career change into a new sector.
